# 7shifts Brand Voice

> How the 7shifts marketing team writes. The **company** voice, shared by everyone, and the single source for verbal voice (the brand-guidelines doc points here). Distinct from any individual's personal writing voice. Justin's `justin-voice` skill is his, for his own posts and memos. This is 7shifts'.
>
> Pull this into any human-facing writing: campaigns, emails, landing pages, social, blog, readouts. Built from the Winter 2024 brand system (Fuzzco) + the content team's writing guidelines.

## Voice (never changes)

**Knowledgeable. Clear. Warm. Helpful.**

- **Knowledgeable:** built for restaurants, by restaurant people. We understand the real, specific challenges operators face.
- **Clear:** straight to the point. Straightforward guidance, no jargon, to help operators save time and money. Aim for a Grade 8 reading level.
- **Warm:** human, optimistic, relatable. A real person passionate about helping hospitality pros.
- **Helpful:** service-oriented and generous. Every piece gives practical value a reader can use today.

The approach is **B2B service journalism**: empathetic, practical, data-backed, useful. Never fluff, never abstract corporate jargon.

## The one rule above all others

**The operator and the staff are the hero. 7shifts is the trustworthy sidekick.** On your side, by your side. Lead with their outcome, never our feature list. If a sentence makes 7shifts the hero, rewrite it.

## How we talk

- **Talk like a human.** Conversational. Use contractions (don't, it's, you're). Active voice. Second person (you/your).
- **Be a peer, not a lecturer.** Experienced peer, not a teacher talking down. Say "proven methods" or "tips that work," not "best practices."
- **Field-tested authenticity.** Write like someone who's been in the restaurant, not a tech writer looking in. Reference real pain without over-explaining it. Include the small operational details only insiders know, and the messy reality, not just the ideal.

## Tone by audience

| Audience | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Owners/operators** (primary) | Straightforward, confident, action-oriented. Focus on ROI, time savings, cost control. | "Easy to use, easier to love. Make your first schedule for free." |
| **Employees** (secondary) | Playful, warm, conversational. | "Knives down: it's break time." |
| **In-product, to operators** | Straightforward, action-oriented. | "Attention needed. Review and resolve the scheduling conflicts below." |
| **About ourselves** | Reassuring, clear-cut. | "We're here to support your entire restaurant team." |

When you don't know the audience, ask. Tone shifts meaningfully between operators and employees.

## Tone calibration scale (aim for a consistent 3)

1. Too casual: "Yo, scheduling is straight-up crazy during the holidays, amirite? 😅"
2. Too informal: "Okay so like, here's the deal with labor costs. They're kind of a big deal lol."
3. **TARGET — peer-to-peer professional:** "Labor costs hit different during the holidays. You're scheduling more bodies for higher volume, but you're competing with every other restaurant doing the same thing."
4. Too formal: "Holiday scheduling presents several unique challenges for restaurant operators, particularly regarding labor cost management."
5. Too corporate: "The holiday season necessitates comprehensive workforce planning strategies to optimize labor allocation."

## "We always sound clear" (calibration)

- Not enough: "Improve your restaurant operations with better labor cost management."
- **Just right: "Save time and money with team scheduling and payroll."**
- Too much: "Schedule and pay your team."

### Done vs Good

| Context | 🚫 Done (bad) | ✅ Good (7shifts) |
|---|---|---|
| Headline | "Why Your Scheduling System Is Destroying Your Business" (clickbait) | "Is Your Restaurant Scheduling System Costing You? 5 Signs It's Time for an Upgrade" |
| Intro | "In this article, we will discuss how scheduling affects your bottom line." | "Better restaurant scheduling can cut your labor costs by 10%. Here's how." |
| CTA | "Boost team collaboration and transparency." | "Overwhelmed handling team texts and calls? Collaboration is easy with our free team communication app." |
| Word choice | "Leverage our robust, seamless, all-in-one platform to optimize labor management." | "Use 7shifts to make scheduling and managing your team easier. It connects every part of your restaurant." |

## 🚫 Banned words (do not use)

Seamless · All-in-one (as a descriptor of 7shifts) · Labor management · Innovative · Streamline · Synergy · Optimize · Interface · Leverage · Robust · Functionality · Cutting-edge · Best-in-class · Best practices

Also avoid: corporate buzzwords (ecosystem, empower); hedge words (maybe, possibly, might, somewhat); intensifiers (very, really, extremely, absolutely); vague descriptors (good, nice, great, important). And no AI tells: "in today's fast-paced world," "game-changing," em dashes.

**Use instead:** "proven methods," "make easier," "simplify," "connect," "bring together."

## Word selection

- Anglo-Saxon over Latinate (help not assist, use not utilize)
- Concrete over abstract (schedule not framework)
- Specific numbers over vague quantities (three servers not adequate staffing)
- Active verbs over verb-noun combos (decide not make a decision)
- Restaurant terms used naturally (covers, stations, mise, walk-ins, tickets, in the weeds, 86, knives down, last call, tip-out, FOH/BOH). One per piece, not three. Don't over-explain basics.

## Style mechanics

- **American English** (labor not labour, organize not organise)
- **AP Style, with one exception: use the Oxford comma.** "Save time, reduce costs, and improve team communication."
- **Numbers:** spell out zero through nine; numerals for 10+; always numerals with % ("8%," not "eight percent")
- **Acronyms:** spell out on first use (quick service restaurant (QSR))
- **Headlines:** Title Case, specific and promise-driven, not clickbait
- **Headings (H2/H3):** Sentence case
- **Paragraphs:** 2-4 sentences max. Vary sentence length for rhythm. Short sentences for impact. White space between dense sections.
- **Intros:** pass the "So what?" test immediately. Never "In this article, we will..." or "Let's explore..."

## Analogies

Draw from universal experience (sports, cooking, physical objects, weather, household tasks). Use one and move on, don't extend it. Connect to something tangible.
- ✅ "Your prep list is like a recipe. It only works if you follow the steps in order."
- 🚫 "Scheduling is like conducting a symphony orchestra where each musician represents a unique staffing need..." (overextended)

## Mentioning 7shifts (especially in editorial/blog)

- Only when it genuinely solves the specific problem being discussed
- Explain the manual/hard way first, then position 7shifts as the tool that enables the strategy, not the strategy itself
- Maximum once per article, usually near the end
- Never lead with the product, make the article about it, use feature lists, or add sales CTAs

✅ "Tracking this manually means spreadsheets, a calculator, and about 30 minutes per schedule. Or you can use software like 7shifts that calculates labor costs in real time as you build."
🚫 "7shifts is the best scheduling solution for restaurants, offering robust features and intuitive design."

## Sanctioned statements (use these, or write in their key)

- Great teams make great restaurants
- Building successful restaurants, one shift at a time
- You support your team, we support you
- Restaurants are chaotic, 7shifts isn't
- We just 86'd kitchen commotion
- People-powered, every step of the way

## Accuracy safeguards (non-negotiable for published content)

- Cite the primary source for any statistic, claim, or quote. Sources recent (ideally within 2 years).
- Never fabricate stats, labor-law requirements, case studies, quotes, cost figures, or product features.
- Flag for human review: any legal/compliance info, regulatory requirements, financial formulas, food safety.
- Labor law varies by state. Don't make blanket legal claims. Add "check your state and local requirements" and point to official .gov resources.
- When uncertain, use ranges and qualifiers ("typically," "often") instead of false precision.

## Final review checklist

1. Passes the "So what?" test in the first paragraph
2. Warm, clear, helpful, knowledgeable
3. American English + Oxford comma
4. Zero banned words
5. Written for a busy operator at a Grade 8 level
6. Actionable, instructive advice
7. 7shifts positioned as the team-centric platform, mentioned only if relevant
8. All external data cited with a direct link
