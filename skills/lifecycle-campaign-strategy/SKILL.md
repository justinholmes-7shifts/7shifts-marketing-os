# Lifecycle & Campaign Strategy

The thinking layer above execution. It decides what deserves to be built, for whom, why the customer would care, and how we'll know it worked. It does not produce copy, briefs, or deliverables, it produces pressure-tested decisions those flow from. Applies to new campaigns and to in-flight ones (evolve, retire, or rebuild).

Pull the customer truth from the brain as you go: `context/icp.md`, `context/personas.md`, `context/positioning.md`, and the shared vocabulary in `context/framework.md`.

## Foundational principle

**Start with the customer. Why would they care?** Every decision is tested against this. Not "what do we want to say" or "what does the business need," but why would this audience, at this moment, care. If the reason is unclear, fuzzy, or "because we built the feature," go back to the start. This is the operating filter, not a value statement.

## Operating rules

1. **Meet the audience where they are, not where the campaign wants them.** Match the ask to the audience's state. That mismatch is the single biggest reason campaigns underperform.
2. **Every campaign ladders to one business outcome** in one sentence (revenue, paid conversion, attach rate, retention, qualified pipeline). Awareness and engagement are leading indicators, not outcomes.
3. **Capacity is the constraint, not ideas.** Every build means another doesn't. Strategy means saying no.
4. **Measurement is a prerequisite, not a post-launch task.** No measurement plan, no run.
5. **Tier reflects effort, not importance.** Choose tier by what the strategy requires, not by what feels ambitious.
6. **Plain language always.** If it needs translating for Sales, Data, PMM, or leadership, it's marketing-ese, not strategy.

## The campaign design framework (problem first, channel last)

Channels are the last decision, not the first.

1. **Name the problem** in one sentence. What customer behavior or business outcome is broken or missing.
2. **Name the audience** in one sentence, with eligibility criteria.
3. **Name the business outcome** in one sentence. Success for the business, not for marketing.
4. **Name the customer outcome** in one sentence: why they would care, in their terms. If it's "they hear about our feature," it's not ready. (This is the value-prop standard in `context/positioning.md`: name the metric, not the functionality.)
5. **Define the measurement plan:** one primary metric, two or three secondary, baseline, target, read-back date, incrementality method.
6. **Choose tier and channel mix** (now, and only now). If channels don't match the audience's behavior, redo step 2.
7. **Map dependencies and risks** before commitment: what other teams must deliver, what data must be reliable, what this conflicts with, what the failure mode looks like.

## The pressure test (run before any campaign goes to build)

Every campaign answers all of these clearly. "We'll figure it out later" is a fail.

1. Why would the customer care? In their words. **Non-negotiable, fail this one and the campaign fails outright.**
2. What's the one-sentence business outcome? Concrete metric.
3. Who is the audience and why them, not someone else? Specific, sized, in the right state for the ask.
4. What's the customer's job-to-be-done this maps to?
5. What's the one action we're asking for? One, not three.
6. How will we measure it? Metric, baseline, target, timeline, incrementality.
7. What other campaigns target this audience right now? Conflict and suppression check.
8. What does it cost in capacity, not just budget?
9. What does it displace? If this gets built, what doesn't.
10. What's the failure mode and how would we detect it?
11. What's the kill condition?

Survives all 11: worth building. Fails three or more: back to design.

## Existing campaigns: evolve, retire, or rebuild

Existing campaigns aren't entitled to keep running. Capacity spent on a stale campaign is capacity not spent on something better.

- **Evolve:** one sharp change to a single variable (audience, message, channel, timing), measured against current performance.
- **Rebuild:** wrong shape entirely, start from step 1.
- **Retire:** hitting target but no longer strategic, or unreadable. Most teams over-rebuild and under-retire.
